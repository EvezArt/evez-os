### Pony Express Station Confirmation

Millersville was a **Home Station** on the Pony Express route, located between Church Butte and Fort Bridger in western Wyoming.

**Source: National Pony Express Association — Wyoming Stations List**
- URL: https://nationalponyexpress.org/wyoming-stations/
- Route sequence (west to east): ... → Green River → Michael Martin's → Ham's Fork → Rock Ridge → **Church Butte / Church Buttes** → **(H) Millersville** → Fort Bridger / Camp Scott → Muddy → Quaking Aspen → Hanging Rock → Bear River / Briggs
- **(H) = Home Station** — Home Stations were located approximately every 50 miles, provided meals and overnight lodging for riders, and had telegraph stations. This was NOT a minor swing station — Millersville was a significant stop.

**Source: Wikipedia — Church Buttes article**
- URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church_Buttes
- The Church Buttes article explicitly lists "Millersville Station Site" as the next Pony Express stop after Church Buttes, with a redlink: https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Millersville_Station_Site&action=edit&redlink=1
- Citation from article: Guthrie, Carol; Smith, Bart (2009), *The Pony Express: An Illustrated History*, Two Dot Series, Globe Pequot, p. 90, ISBN 978-0762748167.

**Source: Wikipedia — Pony Express article**
- URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pony_Express
- The Pony Express operated from April 3, 1860 to October 26, 1861 (18 months). It had approximately 190 stations spaced 10-15 miles apart along a ~1,900-mile route from St. Joseph, Missouri to Sacramento, California. The route passed through Fort Bridger in Wyoming.

### Geographic & Historical Context

- **Location:** Uinta County, Wyoming, between Church Buttes (~41°30'20"N 110°08'06"W) and Fort Bridger (~41°19'07"N 110°23'11"W)
- **Route:** The Pony Express followed the Oregon/Mormon Trail route through this section — from Fort Bridger, it went northeast toward Millersville, then to Church Buttes, then to Ham's Fork, then to Green River.
- **Church Buttes coordinates:** 41°30′20″N 110°08′06″W (41.50551°N 110.13487°W)
- **Fort Bridger coordinates:** 41°19′7″N 110°23′11″W (41.31861°N 110.38639°W)
- **Approximate Millersville position:** Between these two points, likely along the Blacks Fork of the Green River, possibly near present-day Lyman or Urie, Wyoming.

### Connection to Union Pacific Railroad

- The Pony Express route through this area followed the same general corridor that the Union Pacific Railroad would later use (completed 1869).
- The Overland Stage and Mail (1862-1869) also used this route, running from Green River to Fort Bridger via Church Buttes.
- When the transcontinental railroad was completed in 1869, the Pony Express and stage lines became obsolete. Millersville Station, like many stage/Pony Express stops not directly on the railroad, would have been abandoned.
- The Union Pacific Railroad reached Evanston (approximately 25 miles southwest of Fort Bridger) in November 1868 and was completed to Promontory Summit, Utah on May 10, 1869.

## 2. CHURCH BUTTES GAS PLANT

### Overview

The Church Buttes Gas Plant is a NOAA climate station with records from **1955 to present** — one of the longest-running weather stations in southwestern Wyoming.

### Climate Data (NOAA)

**Source: Wikipedia — Church Buttes article (climate data table)**
- URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church_Buttes
- Climate data label: "Church Buttes Gas Plant, Wyoming, 1991–2020 normals, extremes 1955–present"
- NOAA Station ID: USC00481736
- Record high: 100°F (37.8°C) on June 24, 1985
- Record low: −40°F (−40.0°C) on December 21, 1990
- Annual mean temperature: 43.2°F (6.2°C)
- Annual precipitation: 7.87 inches (201 mm)
- Annual snowfall: 30.8 inches (78 cm)

**NOAA Data Sources:**
- NOWData — NWS Salt Lake City: https://www.weather.gov/wrh/climate?wfo=slc
- NOAA Climate Normals: https://www.ncei.noaa.gov/access/services/data/v1?dataset=normals-monthly-1991-2020&stations=USC00481736&format=pdf&dataTypes=MLY-TMAX-NORMAL,MLY-TMIN-NORMAL,MLY-TAVG-NORMAL,MLY-PRCP-NORMAL,MLY-SNOW-NORMAL
- NOAA National Centers for Environmental Information: https://www.ncei.noaa.gov/

### Location

- **Coordinates:** 41°30′20″N 110°08′06″W (41.50551°N 110.13487°W) — this is the Church Buttes formation itself
- The gas plant is in the vicinity of the Church Buttes landform
- Located in Uinta County, Wyoming
- Approximately 10 miles southwest of Granger, Wyoming
- Near the confluence of Blacks Fork and Hams Fork rivers

### The Church Buttes Landform

**Source: WyoHistory.org — Church Butte article**
- URL: https://www.wyohistory.org/encyclopedia/church-butte
- Author: Tom Rea (May 21, 2018)
- Church Butte is a "soft sandstone and clay landform standing alone to the left of the Oregon-California Trail"
- Rises ~100 feet above surrounding prairie
- Named because Mormon companies held church services there
- Howard Stansbury (Army officer) called it "Church Hill" on Sept. 12, 1850
- Albert Carrington (Stansbury's expedition) described it as "an isolated cliff of brown & green indurated clay, & brown sandstone worn by the elements into fantastical shapes, as minarets, spires, aisles etc. capped by domes"
- John Boardman (1843 Oregon emigrant) called it "Solomon's Temple"
- Mariett Cummings (1852) called it "Echo Rock"
- Richard Burton (1860 British travel writer) compared it to "a ruinous Gothic cathedral"
- The butte was a Pony Express relay station site (April 3, 1860 – October 26, 1861)
- Also used by Overland Stage and Mail (1862-1869)
- "Except for nearby gas wells, the area is now deserted"
- "At one time a busy service station stood just across the road"

- The Wyoming oil and gas industry has been active in Uinta County since the late 19th century

## 3. BEAR RIVER CITY, WYOMING

### The Ghost Town That Lived for One Year (1867-1868)

**Source: Wikipedia — Bear River City, Wyoming**
- URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bear_River_City,_Wyoming
- Coordinates: 41°10′23″N 110°52′12″W (41.1730°N 110.87012°W)
- Location: ~10 miles southeast of Evanston, Wyoming, on the Overland Trail and Emigrant Trail
- In 1867, it was in the Utah Territory (Wyoming Territory wasn't created until May 1869)
- County: Uinta County, Wyoming
- Also known as: "Beartown" (per List of Ghost Towns in Wyoming)
- Classification: Ghost town (abandoned site)

### Origins and Growth

- Traces origins to the early 1860s
- Joseph F. Nounnan, a businessman from Salt Lake City, was contracted to construct the Union Pacific railroad grade where it crossed the Bear River in southwestern Wyoming
- Nounnan constructed a supply depot and lodging for his men on the Overland Stage route, along the California and Mormon emigrant trails
- Due to its excellent location, the city grew rapidly
- At its peak, the town had:
  - Its own newspaper office
  - A red light district
  - Served as a passover for miners, railroad workers, and hunters heading west

### The Bear River City Riot — November 19, 1868

**Source: Wikipedia — Bear River City, Wyoming**
**Source: Wikipedia — Thomas J. Smith (lawman)** — URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_J._Smith_(lawman)
**Source: Legends of America** — URL: https://www.legendsofamerica.com/we-tomsmith/

**The Riot Sequence:**
1. A murder suspect who worked for the railroad was **lynched by vigilantes**
2. Friends of the lynched man **revolted against the vigilantes**
3. The town erupted in violence — several hundred rioters involved
4. Town Marshal Thomas J. Smith (recently appointed) took a stand against both factions
5. Numerous shootouts occurred
6. Almost the entire town was **torched**, including most government buildings
7. Town citizens repelled an assault on the town jail — numerous rioters killed, one citizen (Steve Stokes) killed
8. US Cavalry was dispatched from Fort Bridger
9. **Martial law was imposed**
10. **Total killed: 16 people**

**About Marshal Thomas J. "Bear River" Smith:**
- Born: June 12, 1830 (some sources say 1840), New York City
- Was a professional middleweight boxer before becoming a lawman
- Also worked for the Union Pacific Railroad as a teamster
- Appointed marshal of Bear River City
- His stand during the riot earned him the nickname "Bear River" Smith
- After Bear River City was deserted, he moved to Kit Carson, Colorado, then became marshal of Abilene, Kansas (appointed June 1870)
- In Abilene, he was known as "No Gun Marshal" — banned weapons in town, used his fists
- **Killed November 2, 1870** in Abilene — shot in chest by Andrew McConnell, then nearly decapitated with an axe by Moses Miles
- He was replaced as Abilene marshal by "Wild Bill" Hickok
- President Dwight D. Eisenhower considered Smith a personal hero
- Portrayed by Ronald Reagan in Death Valley Days episode "Marshall 'Bear River' Smith"

**Note on date discrepancy:** Wikipedia's Thomas J. Smith article says the riot was in 1867, while the Bear River City article says November 19, 1868. The Bear River City article's date is more specific and likely more accurate, as it names the exact day. However, the Legends of America source also says 1867. The Wikipedia List of Ghost Towns in Wyoming lists Bear River City as settled 1867 and abandoned — matching the 1867-1868 lifespan.

### Why It Was Abandoned

- The riot "essentially ended any future the small town might have had"
- The town was largely burned to the ground
- Martial law was imposed until the railroad track layers had passed
- "Bear River City soon became deserted, another railroad ghost town"
- The town existed for approximately ONE YEAR (1867-1868) — a classic "hell-on-wheels" railroad construction camp

### Connection to Union Pacific

- Joseph F. Nounnan was contracted to construct the Union Pacific railroad grade in the area
- The town existed to serve railroad construction workers
- The riot involved railroad employees
- US Cavalry from Fort Bridger (a Union Pacific-adjacent military post) was dispatched to quell the riot
- The town was abandoned once the railroad construction moved west

## 4. PIEDMONT, WYOMING

### The Ghost Town Where Steven Found "Breathable Air"

**Source: Wikipedia — Piedmont, Wyoming**
- URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piedmont,_Wyoming
- Coordinates: 41°13′4″N 110°37′40″W (41.21778°N 110.62778°W)
- County: Uinta County, Wyoming
- Classification: Ghost town (abandoned)
- Also known as: "Byrne" (original name)
- Location: Southeast of Evanston, Wyoming, near Fort Bridger

### Founding by Mormon Pioneers

- Settled circa **1867** to provide railroad ties for the Union Pacific Railroad
- **Moses Byrne** built several kilns for producing charcoal
- **Charles Guild** established one of the first ranches in the Territory
- Both Byrne and Guild were **Mormon pioneers**
- The Guild family joined the Byrne family in **1866** at the Muddy River Station in southwest Wyoming, having traveled from Salt Lake City

### Naming

- Originally named "Byrne" after Moses Byrne
- Renamed to "Piedmont" due to confusion with Bryan Station (west of Green River)
- Both of Byrne's wives (Anne Beus and Catherine Cardon) and Guild's wife (Marie Madeleine Cardon) were from small towns in the Province of Turin, part of the **Piedmont region of northern Italy**
- Catherine Cardon Byrne and Marie Madeleine Cardon Guild were **sisters**

### Railroad History

- As the transcontinental railroad moved into western Wyoming, a wood and water station was needed
- A spot ~5 miles west of Muddy River Station was ideal, situated in the direct line of the track
- Moses Byrne was asked to run the station
- Originally a tent camp for railroad graders constructing the roadbed up the steep mountain to Aspen Station
- The route had many sharp curves, including a full horseshoe bend
- By 1868, the railroad crew arrived to lay track
- Helper engines were needed on the 8-mile grade
- Wells provided plentiful water
- Sidings, engine shed, and water tank were constructed
- Piedmont became a **wood and water refueling station for helper engines**

### Charcoal Kilns

**Source: Wikipedia — Piedmont Charcoal Kilns State Historic Site**
- URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piedmont_Charcoal_Kilns
- Moses Byrne constructed **5 charcoal kilns** in Piedmont in **1877** (some sources say 1869)
- Cost: $1,500 total
- Built of local sandstone, beehive-shaped, ~30 feet in circumference and ~30 feet high, with 24-inch-thick walls
- Quaking aspen and pine logs were hauled by ox teams to the kilns where they were burned into charcoal
- The Union Pacific Railroad Company used the charcoal as fuel for passenger cars
- Most charcoal was shipped to the Salt Lake City area for use in small smelters and blacksmith shops
- **2 kilns have been destroyed**; 3 survive
- The **Piedmont Charcoal Kilns were listed on the National Register of Historic Places in 1971** (June 3, 1971, NRHP reference No. 71000894)
- Managed by the state of Wyoming as **Piedmont Charcoal Kilns State Historic Site**
- NRHP Nomination: Barnhart, Bill (1971). "Piedmont Charcoal Kilns." National Park Service.

### Town Life at Peak

- "A typical tent camp for the railroad, probably at this time knew its greatest population"
- Evidence of only approximately **twenty homes**
- The tent town served as a base camp for graders constructing the roadbed
- Men were needed to run helper engines, so more families moved in
- Homesteaders also arrived
- The Guilds opened a **mercantile establishment**
- The town boasted **4 saloons**
- Soldiers from nearby Fort Bridger frequented the saloons
- There was a general store, two-story hotel, school, post office, and newspaper

**Source: GhostTowns.com** — URL: https://www.ghosttowns.com/states/wy/piedmont.html
- "Piedmont was established near the small Union Pacific railroad station of Byrne in 1869 and was later renamed to Piedmont"
- "When the Aspen Tunnel was built in 1901, the town died as the railroad no longer came through"
- "Today about a dozen structures remain in addition to some charcoal kilns"
- "The town is on a working ranch and visitors are not welcome"

### The Railroad Bypass and Decline (~1910)

- **About 1910**, the Union Pacific Railroad began digging the **Aspen Tunnel** through Aspen Mountain
- The tunnel was approximately **1.5 miles long**
- The tunnel eliminated the steep, winding grade from Piedmont to Aspen Station
- The railroad was **rerouted from LeRoy to the tunnel**, missing Piedmont by several miles
- **Piedmont was stranded**, and its demise began

Note: The GhostTowns.com source says the Aspen Tunnel was built in 1901, while Wikipedia says "about 1910." The tunnel construction likely began earlier, with the rerouting completed around 1910.

### Final Decline

- **1940:** Lack of business forced the closing of the old Guild Mercantile Store
- Since then, most of the buildings have been hauled away
- **All that remains:**
  - Three or four tumbledown remnants of homes
  - Some foundations
  - The coal dump where the engine shed once stood
  - The charcoal kilns of Moses Byrne
  - The cemeteries

### Coal Mining History

- Piedmont's primary industry was **logging and charcoal production**, not coal mining per se
- However, coal was mined nearby at **Almy, Wyoming** (a few miles north of Evanston)
  - Almy was a coal mining camp along the Bear River for 5 miles
  - Named for James T. Almy of the Rocky Mountain Coal Company
  - 4,000 residents at peak
  - Three major mine explosions: 1881 (38 killed), 1886 (13 killed), 1895 (62 killed — third worst mining disaster in Wyoming history)
  - The Union Pacific Coal Company (subsidiary of Union Pacific Railroad) had an ownership stake in one of Almy's mines
  - 4-mile Almy Spur line connected to the Union Pacific Railroad
  - Mines shut down in the 1940s
  - Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almy,_Wyoming

### Current Status

- **Population:** 0 (ghost town, abandoned)
- **Remaining structures:** ~12 structures (per GhostTowns.com), including 3-4 tumbledown home remnants, foundations, charcoal kilns, coal dump, cemeteries
- **Charcoal kilns:** 3 surviving kilns, listed on National Register of Historic Places (1971), managed as Piedmont Charcoal Kilns State Historic Site
- **Access:** On a working ranch; "visitors are not welcome" (per GhostTowns.com)
- **Nearest city:** Fort Bridger, Wyoming (population 354 per 2020 census)

## 5. CONTEXTUAL CONNECTIONS

### The Uinta County Corridor

All four locations exist in a narrow geographic corridor in Uinta County, Wyoming:

```
West ←→ East (approximate)
Granger (98 pop) → Church Buttes (~41.5°N) → Millersville (lost) → Fort Bridger (354 pop) → Piedmont (0 pop) → Bear River City (0 pop) → Evanston (11,747 pop)
```

### The Transportation Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1843 | Jim Bridger & Louis Vasquez establish Fort Bridger |
| 1847 | Brigham Young's party camps at Church Buttes (July 7) |
| 1856 | Granger stage station built (stone and adobe) |
| 1860-1861 | Pony Express operates — Church Buttes & Millersville are stations |
| 1862-1869 | Overland Stage and Mail uses same route |
| 1867 | Piedmont settled by Mormon pioneers (Moses Byrne, Charles Guild) |
| 1867 | Bear River City founded by Joseph F. Nounnan for UP construction |
| Nov 1868 | Union Pacific reaches Evanston; Bear River City Riot (Nov 19) |
| 1868-1869 | Wahsatch tunnel construction camp (Utah side) |
| May 1869 | Transcontinental railroad completed — Pony Express & stages obsolete |
| 1869 | Moses Byrne builds charcoal kilns at Piedmont |
| 1877 | Byrne builds 5 charcoal kilns (per NRHP nomination) |
| ~1910 | Union Pacific bypasses Piedmont with Aspen Tunnel |
| 1940 | Guild Mercantile Store closes — Piedmont effectively dead |
| 1955-present | Church Buttes Gas Plant NOAA weather station active |
| 1971 | Piedmont Charcoal Kilns listed on National Register of Historic Places |

### The Union Pacific Thread

Every single location in this dossier is connected to the **Union Pacific Railroad**:
- **Millersville** — Pony Express/Overland Stage stop on the route UP would later parallel
- **Church Buttes** — Pony Express relay station, now the site of a gas plant in UP territory
- **Bear River City** — Founded specifically for UP railroad grade construction; destroyed by riot involving UP workers
- **Piedmont** — Founded to serve UP as a wood/water station for helper engines; killed when UP rerouted through Aspen Tunnel

### The Spectral Geography

What makes this corridor haunting is the **layering of abandonment**:
1. **Pony Express stations** (1860-1861) → abandoned when telegraph completed
2. **Overland Stage stations** (1862-1869) → abandoned when railroad completed
3. **Railroad construction camps** (1867-1869) → abandoned when track moved west (Bear River City)
4. **Railroad service towns** (1867-1910) → abandoned when railroad was rerouted (Piedmont)
5. **Gas extraction infrastructure** (1955-present) → the only thing still operating, silently, at Church Buttes

The land keeps being used and abandoned. Each wave of industry leaves its ghosts. The Church Buttes formation itself — compared to cathedrals by 19th-century travelers — watches over all of it, eroding so slowly that photos from the 1860s look the same as today.
