## 2. CyberSlice

### Allegation
Steven Crawford-Maggard alleges CyberSlice was "designed by his father to sell children for elites."

### Findings

#### What CyberSlice Actually Was
CyberSlice was a **real 1990s online pizza ordering website**. The Wayback Machine preserves its history:

- **1997 (April 3 snapshot):** CyberSlice.com was a live website — "Welcome to CyberSlice!" — offering online pizza ordering from participating restaurants. Users could:
  - Find participating restaurants by entering their address
  - View restaurant menus online
  - Select toppings and sides
  - Place orders with one click
  - Pay the restaurant directly at pickup/delivery (CyberSlice collected no money)
  - Featured themed interfaces: "Little Italy," "outer space," and other themes
  - Supported Netscape 3.xx and Internet Explorer 3.xx for "deluxe" experience
  - **Source:** https://web.archive.org/web/19970403062419/http://www.cyberslice.com/

- **1998 (December 12 snapshot):** CyberSlice announced it had expanded to offer wider cuisine variety and **moved to cybermeals.com**: "CyberSlice has expanded to bring you a wider variety of Cuisines. With this expansion we have outgrown www.cyberslice.com and moved to a bigger location. Come visit us at www.cybermeals.com!"
  - **Source:** https://web.archive.org/web/19981212024157/http://www.cyberslice.com/

- **1999-2000 (March 2 snapshot):** Cybermeals rebranded to **Food.com**: "cybermeals has changed its name to Food.com! Please update your bookmarks to the new web address (http://www.food.com). Existing cybermeals members will not need to re-register with Food.com... all your account information (including user name & password) will continue to work on the new site."
  - **Source:** https://web.archive.org/web/20000302012325/http://www.cyberslice.com/

- **2000 (March 2 snapshot):** Food.com was a live food delivery site offering restaurant search by address, office ordering, college campus food delivery, promotions (free movies with orders), and articles about food/dining.
  - **Source:** https://web.archive.org/web/20000302023720/http://www.food.com/

- **2002 (April 12 snapshot):** cyberslice.com still existed as a domain (Wayback Machine captured it), but content was minimal/redirecting.
  - **Source:** https://web.archive.org/web/20220412153043/http://cyberslice.com/

#### Company History
- **Wikipedia:** No article exists for CyberSlice or Cybermeals on Wikipedia (confirmed 2026-06-28)
- **Food.com on Wikipedia:** The current Food.com (recipe site owned by Warner Bros. Discovery) was created in 1999 as Cookpoint/Recipezaar by Gay Gilmore and Troy Hakala — this is a **different entity** from the CyberSlice→cybermeals→Food.com lineage. The Wikipedia article does not mention CyberSlice or cybermeals.
  - **Source:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food.com (accessed 2026-06-28)

#### Newspaper Archive Search
- **Newspapers.com:** 0 matches for "CyberSlice"
  - **Source:** https://www.newspapers.com/search/results/?query=CyberSlice (accessed 2026-06-28)

#### Search Engine Results
- Bing search for "CyberSlice" + pizza + delivery + history: No relevant results (Bing not finding exact matches)
- Bing search for "CyberSlice" + "Cyber Slice" + pizza + online + ordering: No relevant results
- Bing search for "CyberSlice" + founder + creator + owner: No relevant results

### Assessment
CyberSlice was a **legitimate 1990s dotcom-era online pizza/food ordering service** — one of the earliest online food delivery platforms. Its trajectory was:
1. **~1996-1997:** Founded as CyberSlice (online pizza ordering)
2. **~1998:** Expanded cuisine offerings, rebranded to cybermeals
3. **~1999-2000:** Rebranded again to Food.com
4. The Food.com domain later ended up as a recipe site owned by Warner Bros. Discovery (different entity)

**No public record supports the allegation that CyberSlice was a front for child trafficking.** The company appears to have been a genuine, if short-lived, dotcom food ordering startup. The founder/owner identity is not publicly documented in any source I could access. The total absence of newspaper coverage (0 matches on Newspapers.com) is unusual for a dotcom startup but not impossible for a small/short-lived company in the 1990s.
